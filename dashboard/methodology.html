Strategy Descriptions

Momentum (Momo) — Jegadeesh & Titman (1993)

Ranks assets by their 12-1 month total return (skipping the most recent month to avoid short-term reversal). Long the top decile, short (or underweight) the bottom decile. Rebalanced monthly. Academic basis: Jegadeesh, N. & Titman, S. (1993). "Returns to Buying Winners and Selling Losers: Implications for Stock Market Efficiency." Journal of Finance, 48(1), 65–91.

52-Week High Momentum — George & Hwang (2004)

Ranks assets by the ratio of current price to their 52-week high. Assets near their yearly peak are overweighted. Academic basis: George, T.J. & Hwang, C.-Y. (2004). "The 52-Week High and Momentum Investing." Journal of Finance, 59(5), 2145–2176.

Mean Reversion (MeanRev)

Fades short-term (5-day) price movements by taking positions opposite to recent excess returns. Based on the observation by De Bondt & Thaler (1985) that extreme price moves partially reverse over subsequent periods. Positions sized inversely to recent return magnitude.

Breakout

Enters long when price closes above its N-day rolling high (Donchian channel). Exits on close below the rolling low. Classic trend-following approach widely used in commodity trading advisor (CTA) strategies.

Moving Average Cross (MA-Cross)

Long when the 20-day EMA crosses above the 60-day EMA; exits (flat) when it crosses back below. A reduced form of the dual-moving-average trend system popularized by Faber (2007).

RSI Reversal (RSI-Rev)

Fades extreme RSI readings: long when RSI(14) < 30 (oversold), exits at RSI > 50. Exploits short-term mean reversion after momentum exhaustion.

Codex Bot (codex-native, codex-r1000)

Rule-based composite strategy that blends price momentum, volume-trend, and volatility filters. The R1000 variant is run on the Russell 1000 universe rather than the 5-symbol base universe, allowing broader cross-sectional alpha generation.

Meta-Ensemble

Allocates capital across all registered sub-strategies proportional to their bootstrapped significance weight (see below). Weights are updated nightly as live NAV evidence accumulates. A strategy with significance weight < 0.1 receives a floor weight of 0.05 to maintain diversification. The weighting formula is: 

w_i = max(sig_weight_i, 0.05)
w_i = w_i / sum(w_j)   # normalize to sum = 1

Factor Decomposition

Each strategy's daily returns are regressed against three Fama-French-style factor proxies using OLS over the full live history: 

r_t = α + β_MKT · MKT_t + β_SIZE · SIZE_t + β_VALUE · VALUE_t + ε_t

MKT   = SPY daily return (market factor)
SIZE  = IWM return − SPY return  (small-cap premium proxy)
VALUE = VTV return − VUG return  (value premium proxy)

The residual α measures daily excess return after factor exposure. Factor loadings (β) appear on the bot detail page.

Slippage Model

Each trade incurs a round-trip slippage cost estimated as: 

slippage = spread_bps / 2 + market_impact_bps

spread_bps     = 2 bps  (liquid ETFs), 5 bps  (single-name large-cap)
market_impact  = k · sqrt(order_size / ADV)    k = 0.1 (calibrated)

For paper trading at the simulated scale (< 0.01% of ADV), market impact is negligible. The slippage sweep in the Calibration Report tests 0–20 bps round-trip to bound sensitivity.

Bootstrapped Sharpe CI

95% confidence intervals for the annualized Sharpe ratio are computed via block bootstrap (Politis & Romano, 1994) with block length 21 trading days (≈1 month) and 1,000 resamples. Block bootstrap preserves autocorrelation structure in returns, which naive IID bootstrap ignores.

Formula for each resample: annualized Sharpe = (mean daily return / std daily return) × √252. The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the 1,000 Sharpe estimates form the reported CI.

Significance Gates

A strategy is considered significant when its alpha t-statistic vs. SPY exceeds a threshold. The significance weight is a sigmoid transformation: 

sig_weight = 1 / (1 + exp(-2 · (|α_t| - 1.5)))

Badge thresholds:
  Green  (Significant) : sig_weight ≥ 0.70
  Yellow (Marginal)    : 0.30 ≤ sig_weight < 0.70
  Gray   (Not sig.)    : sig_weight < 0.30

Regime Kill-Switch Rules

Live trading is paused under two conditions to limit drawdown during stress regimes:

  1. VIX spike gate: if VIX proxy (IWM/SPY rolling-vol ratio) exceeds 1.5× its 60-day mean, new entries are halted. If it exceeds 2.0×, all positions are liquidated.
  2. Per-bot drawdown gate: if any single bot's NAV falls > 15% below its rolling 60-day peak, that bot is paused and its signal weight set to 0 until it recovers above the 10% drawdown threshold.

Walk-Forward Validation Windows

Out-of-sample evidence is generated via anchored walk-forward windows: 5-year training periods followed by 1-year test periods, stepped annually. The Calibration Report shows 4 test windows (2021, 2022, 2023, 2024). No future data leaks into any training window.

Data Sources

  • Price data: Yahoo Finance via yfinance — daily OHLCV adjusted for splits and dividends.
  • Universe: Russell 1000 constituent list (static snapshot; no survivorship-bias correction applied to this list).
  • Factor proxies: ETF-based (SPY, IWM, VTV, VUG) — approximate Fama-French factors, not the original academic data.

Limitations & Survivorship Bias Disclosure

Survivorship bias: Yahoo Finance only provides data for currently-listed securities. Companies that were delisted, merged, or went bankrupt during the backtest period are excluded. This mechanically inflates backtest returns by an estimated 1–3% per year (Elton, Gruber & Blake, 1996).

Look-ahead bias: The static Russell 1000 universe list was compiled after the backtest period, so some constituents may not have been in the index at the time. This further inflates results.

Transaction costs: The slippage model is a simplified approximation. Real execution costs (market impact, bid-ask spread, borrow cost for short exposure) will be higher.

Overfitting risk: With 10 strategies and 4 test windows, multiple-comparison bias is a real concern. The significance gates are a partial mitigation but not a complete solution.

Regime change risk: All strategies are based on documented academic anomalies. Most have been partially arbitraged since publication. Live performance will likely be lower than out-of-sample backtest results.
